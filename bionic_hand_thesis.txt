CHAPTER 1: OVERVIEW
1.1. Introduction
Our working and living environment are designed and optimized entirely for the biological structure of humans. From household tools and doorknobs to industrial equipment, everything requires a specific method of interaction. [1] Therefore, for humanoid robots  such as ASIMO (Honda), PR2 (Willow Garage), TORO (DLR), or Tesla Optimus  to assist or replace humans in daily tasks, equipping them with anthropomorphic artificial hands is a prerequisite. [2]
Anthropometric hands here not only have aesthetic significance, but also address three core problems for service robots: (1) Dexterity, (2) Compact size for maneuvering in tight spaces, and (3) Friendliness and safety when interacting directly with humans.
For over a century, roboticists have endeavored to replicate the function and form of the human hand. This approach is logical, as most objects in our living environments are designed for manipulation by the human hand. Therefore, robots intended to handle the same objects should ideally be equipped with an end effector of a similar configuration and dexterity. However, the technology and mechanisms available to robots are fundamentally different from those that living organisms have evolved to depend on. Notably, current artificial hands primarily rely on electric actuators that cannot yet match the power density provided by biological muscles. Consequently, compromises on dexterity, weight. [3]
This thesis aims to address that problem: designing and developing an anthropomorphic robotic hand system using underactuated actuators, integrating 3D printing technology, with the goal of low cost and light weight while still ensuring adaptive grasping capabilities. [4]
1.2. Human Hand Biomechanics and Anatomical Analysis
The human hand has superior gripping properties because of its mixed skeletal and muscular structure, and the dexterity of its tendons. Anthropomorphic multi-fingered dexterous hands, that usually have more than three fingers imitating human hand movements with higher dexterity compared with conventional mechanical grippers, conform to people's operating habits and are suitable for handling tools like a human. An adult human hand possesses 21 to 24 degrees of freedom (DoF), distributed across 15 different joints. [5] [4]Therefore, it has attracted wide academic attention. The design of a multi-fingered dexterous hand should not only simply pursue the imitation of human hand shape, but also be guided by in-depth analysis of the physiological structure and transmission principles of the human hand. [5]
 1.2.1. Skeletal and Joint Kinematics
The design of the anthropomorphic robotic hand is guided by the analysis of the physiological structure and functions of human hands. The skeletal is composed of metacarpals and phalanges. The thumb has three joints, including an interphalangeal (IP) joint, a metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joint, and a carpometacarpal (CMC) joint. The remaining four fingers consist of a distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint, a proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joint, and an MCP joint (see Fig. 1 (a)). In total, the human hand has 15 joints and 21 DOFs. It is owing to the large number of joints and DOFs that human hand can perform dexterous motions. However, implementing all DOFs and full actuation in the robotic hand design would greatly increase the complexity and difficulty of control, so it is necessary to reduce the number of actuated DOFs. Extensive research has shown a strong link between the movements of the DIP and PIP joints. In other words, when the PIP joint flexes, the DIP joint flexes as well with a proportional bending angle to the PIP joint. Therefore, the robotic finger can be designed as an underactuated form where DIP and PIP joints share one DOF. Owing to the complex anatomy and movements of the thumb, there has been a great deal of controversy regarding the CMC and MCP movements. In our study, the movements of the thumb are considered as a combination of flexion/extension and abduction/adduction movements.

Figure 1.1 Anatomy of human hand
The four long fingers (Index, Middle, Ring, Little): Each finger comprises three joints: the metacarpophalangeal (MCP), the proximal interphalangeal (PIP), and the distal interphalangeal (DIP). The MCP provides two degrees of freedom (flexion/extension and abduction/adduction), while the PIP and DIP each provide one degree of freedom (flexion/extension only)[]. Biomechanical studies indicate a strong kinematic coupling between the movements of the PIP and DIP joints: when the PIP flexes, the DIP also flexes automatically at a proportionally related angle.[]

Thumb: The thumb plays a core role, accounting for up to 40% of the hand's overall function. The thumb's anatomy is more complex, with three joints: the interphalangeal (IP) joint, the metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joint, and especially the carpometacarpal (CMC) joint. The CMC is a saddle joint that provides flexion/extension and abduction/adduction movements, allowing the thumb to be in opposition to the other four fingers. Humans have the highest opposability index among primates. [6]
 Design Consequence: The kinematics of the robotic thumb must be designed separately, ensuring a workspace that can intersect with the other fingertips to perform precise pinching maneuvers.
 1.2.2. Hand Arches
The human hand is not a rigid flat surface. Envelope grasping relies heavily on the flexion and extension movements of the hand arch system, including the transverse arches and the longitudinal arches. The distal transverse arch is formed by five metacarpal bones angled towards each other, creating a concave curve in the palm (typically a 10° difference between adjacent bones).

Figure 1.4: Hand Arches
? Design Consequence: Instead of designing a flat robotic hand like traditional industrial grippers, the mechanical design (CAD) of the hand needs to be arch-shaped with a 10° angle between the fingers to increase contact area and stability when grasping cylindrical or spherical objects.
 1.2.3. Muscle-Tendon Actuation Topology
A notable evolutionary feature of humans is the spatial distribution of muscle groups. The force of flexion/extension of the fingers does not originate from the palm, but primarily from large forearm flexor/extensor muscles. This force is transmitted to the finger joints through a network of long, smooth tendons. Conversely, the palm contains only small intrinsic muscles responsible for fine abduction/adduction movements and posture maintenance. By "relocating" these large muscles away from the hand, the human hand maintains its light weight, slenderness, and low rotational inertia.
Through extensive examination into the mechanics of human hand motion, we observe that muscles housed within the palm and those situated in the forearm contribute approxi-mately equally to this function. Despite their diminutive size, the intrinsic muscles within the palm are pivotal in achieving dexterous manipulation using the fingers. For example, the interosseous muscles embedded in the palm primarily control [1]
? Design Consequence: This mechanical distribution structure directly inspires the mixed arrangement of motors in advanced robotic hands (such as TRX-Hand5 or RoboRay): Power motors are placed in the forearm to mimic forearm flexion, transmitting force via cables (tendon-driven); while miniature servo motors (posture motors) can be placed in the palm to control differential direction. [1] [3]
Summary: The above anatomical analyses establish the golden rules (design guidelines) for the project: (1) The synchronous flexion properties of the PIP/DIP joint must be exploited to reduce the number of motors. (2) Design the palm in an arched shape. (3) Use a cable network instead of a biological tendon to optimize space and simulate the flexibility of the human hand.
1.3. Overview about solutions/ products 
In the design of multi-fingered anthropomorphic robotic hands, transmission mechanisms are a critical factor, directly impacting the system's performance, dexterity, accuracy, and load capacity [2]. To overcome the limitations of traditional two-finger grasping hands, researchers have developed numerous artificial hand prototypes based on different actuation methods. Below is an in-depth analysis of four core approaches currently in use: cable-driven, bar-driven, direct-driven, and hybrid actuation.
 1.3.1. Linkage-based Approach
Operating principle: Unlike cable systems, this method uses rigid mechanical links (usually four-bar linkages) or gears to transmit motion. The characteristic principle of this mechanism is "kinematic coupling." [7] This means that the movement of the proximal interphalangeal joint (PIP) and the distal interphalangeal joint (DIP) is locked together by a fixed physical transmission ratio. A motor (usually rotary or linear cylinder) pushes a main connecting rod, thereby forcing all the finger joints to flex along a mathematically predetermined trajectory. [7] [8]. One of the most complex parts when creating the initial concept of our anthropomorphic robot hand with six actuators is determining the suitable actuators to use. When conceiving a finger movement mechanism, careful consideration should be given to the appropriate actuator that could maximize the potential of the finger mechanism. Similarly, when choosing an actuator, it must be well thought about what kind of suitable finger mechanism could maximize the actuator's potential. Using those considerations, the role of the actuators becomes crucial as it is closely related to the concept of our proposed robot hand and finger mechanism. [8]
Typical examples:
Bebionic Hand: This popular commercial prosthetic arm places DC motors directly in the palm, pushing a rigid linkage system to flex the fingers.

Figure 1.5: Demonstration of prosthetic hand grasping capabilities
OPENGRASP-LITE / MCR-Hand III: These research hands use a hinge mechanism, but are improved by adding "compliant linkages" or integrated springs to absorb impact forces, overcoming traditional rigidity.

Comparison and Evaluation:
Advantages: Excellent mechanical reliability. Extremely high load capacity (large clamping force) and superior repeatability due to the absence of slack or elasticity. Easy to manufacture and maintain using 3D printing. [8] [6]
Disadvantages: Rigid opening and closing trajectory. If a finger joint is caught on an obstruction, the entire finger will lock up, losing the ability to closely grip the object's shape (lack of passive compliance). [6] This mechanism also takes up a lot of space, making the hand bulky.
Operating Principle: The system utilizes a four-bar linkage mechanism to transmit motion from the lead-screw assembly to the phalanges. The core technical feature is the integration of a spring element acting as a parallel elastic link. The amplitude of the spring's deformation is linearly proportional to the finger's flexion angle (Kinematic-dependent deformation). As the actuator reduces the workspace (finger flexion), the spring accumulates potential energy proportional to the depth of the grasping trajectory. 
Evaluation: This design deliberately avoids using springs for shock absorption (Series Elastic Actuation). Instead, it utilizes the spring's potential energy to maintain continuous tension across the mechanical system, effectively eliminating backlash during static transmission. Simultaneously, it provides the required restoring torque for the finger to auto-extend when actuation ceases, optimizing unidirectional control architecture.
 Advanced Analysis Product: OPENGRASP-LITE Robotic Hand Platform
To thoroughly overcome the shortcomings of open-loop control and the lack of dynamic feedback data of traditional servo motors, the open-source OPENGRASP-LITE platform applies an extremely sophisticated mechatronic system architecture, representing the design standard of modern research robotic arms: [7]
Mechanical Architecture: The hand is designed with a 5-finger configuration, possessing 11 degrees of freedom but optimized down to 6 degrees of actuation (6 DoA - 1 motor for each long finger and 2 motors for the thumb). [7] Unlike cable drive systems, this system uses an elastic hinged linkage mechanism combined with rigidly fixed DIP joints. Kinetic energy is transmitted from the motors via a worm gear assembly. The core non-backdrivable characteristic of the worm gear mechanism allows the hand to maintain a firm grip on the object without consuming energy to maintain current, while also preventing motor overheating. [7]
Electrical Architecture: The breakthrough of the system is the complete elimination of commercial servos in favor of integrated gearboxes and magnetic quadrature encoders (Magnetic Quadrature Encoders). [7]To solve the problem of extremely limited space (palm-sized volume of only ~50cmģ), the entire hardware system is compactly embedded into two custom boards (Main board and Drive board). The motor control board uses two ARM Cortex M4F (100MHz) microcontrollers combined with H-bridge drivers to independently power the DC motors. [7] This hardware architecture establishes a perfect foundation for real-time acquisition of angular position and current data.

Figure 1.8: Hardware architecture diagram
Control Architecture: Taking full advantage of the powerful hardware, the system does not use a conventional single-loop position PID controller but applies a Multi-rate Cascaded Control Strategy. The low-level control loop is decomposed into three tightly nested layers: (1) Current loop (using a PI controller) running at an extremely high frequency of 10 kHz; (2) Velocity loop (using a PID controller) running at a frequency of 1 kHz; [7] (3) Position loop (using a P controller) running at a frequency of 1 kHz. In particular, to address the signal delay and noise generated from the inductive characteristics of the motor winding (LR-circuit), the algorithm incorporates an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) filter to smooth the input voltage and current signals before they are used in the calculation. [7]
Core Control Algorithm (Cascade PID)
Within the Approach and Grasping states, the microcontroller executes a Cascade PID Control algorithm. This nested loop structure is the critical mathematical engine that allows a standard DC motor to simulate industrial force feedback. [7]

Figure 1.9: Cascaded PID Control
Outer Position Loop: This loop (primarily utilizing Proportional and Derivative terms) calculates the error between the target angle and the real-time encoder feedback:
e=?_target-?_current
Inner Current/Torque Loop: This high-speed loop receives I_ref and compares it against the actual measured current (I_measured) derived from the shunt resistor. If the finger encounters a rigid obstacle, I_measured surges. The algorithm subsequently applies an Anti-windup/Clamping threshold to the final PWM output, seamlessly shifting from position control to static torque maintenance. [7]
System-Level Operating Procedure (State Machine) (will be replace by control diagrams)
The grasping phase of the end-effector is executed via a sequential Finite State Machine (FSM):
1. Initialization: The MCU configures hardware timers for PWM generation and attaches interrupts to the encoder channels.
2. Calibration (Homing): The actuator retracts the mechanism to its fully open boundary. Upon hitting the mechanical hard-stop, the system detects a sharp current spike (Stall Current), registers this as the absolute Zero point, and resets the encoder pulse counter.
3. Approach: The system receives a target position command (?_target) and drives the finger into flexion at high velocity.
4. Grasping/Holding: Upon physical contact with the object, kinematic resistance induces a current rise. The system transitions into a force-holding state to maintain secure prehension without fracturing the object.

 1.3.2. Tendon-driven Approach
Principle and Characteristics: Tendon-driven systems utilize a network of cables to control the joints, directly mimicking the human tendon and muscle system. This design allows for an extremely compact finger structure, integrating many degrees of freedom (DoF) and achieving very high dexterity. To optimize space, the force-generating motors are often located far away (such as in the forearm) [2]

Figure 1.11:Examples of multi-fingered anthropomorphic hands: (a) Faive Hand [126]; (b) Tesla Optimus Hand [127]; (c) Utah/MIT Dexterous Hand [128].
Notable examples: Popular platforms employing this mechanism include the Utah/MIT Hand, Shadow Dexterous Hand, DLR's Awiwi Hand, and newer commercial designs like the Tesla Optimus Hand. Additionally, underactuated designs like the Pisa/IIT SoftHand utilize cables combined with a differential mechanism to create synergies [9], allowing a single motor to drive multiple joints simultaneously. More recently, the UB Hand IV employs a twisted string actuation mechanism that directly converts the motor's rotational motion into massive linear traction without the need for a gearbox.
 

      Figure 1.12: Twisted tendon actuation method (courtesy Bologna University)
The big advantage of this method lies in the fact that there is a direct transformation of
rotational motion in the motors to linear motion in the tendons  no intermediate pulley
system is required to move the fingers. Another advantage is that large forces can be
exerted by the fingers. Essentially, the tendon pair can continue to be twisted, increasing the
force exerted by the finger  until of course there is a mechanical failure somewhere.
However, this can be a slow way of actuating fingers since many rotations are required in
order to fully close a finger. In their most recent public video it seems the UB hand
researchers have switched to using Servo motors to control fingers for improved speed.
 In-depth analysis of the product: Tendon-Driven Mechanism (Representative Model: Tesla Optimus Gen 2 [10]
Operating Principle: This system rigorously applies biomechanical anatomy principles by entirely decoupling the actuation unit from the manipulation end-effector. A centralized actuation assemblycomprising motors, reduction gearboxes, and winding spoolsis responsible for retracting and releasing high-tensile tendons. The cable routing is geometrically constrained via guide pulleys situated coaxially at the finger joints (MCP, PIP, DIP). In the reverse stroke, the mechanism utilizes an antagonistic spring system at the joints to passively release potential energy, forcing the finger into extension when tendon tension is reduced (Passive Extension).


Evaluation: This architecture decisively resolves the spatial assembly bottleneck. By shifting the mass distribution toward the base of the hand, it minimizes the mass moment of inertia (I =???m_i  r_i^2 ?) at the fingertips. This dynamic characteristic yields high-frequency response capabilities and minimizes energy consumption, establishing it as the most highly optimized topology for contemporary commercial Humanoid Robots.

Figure 1.13: Kinematics model of Tesla Optimus Hand
A. Mechanical Architecture: The Tesla Optimus Hand inherits a tendon-driven design philosophy combined with an underactuated mechanism for manipulation. [10] Moving the actuator assembly away from the palm overcomes stringent spatial constraints, optimizes mass distribution, and significantly reduces mass moment of inertia at the knuckles. Each finger, including the proximal and distal phalanges, is pulled by cables routed along rotating axes. To restore the extended state, the system utilizes the mechanical stiffness of torsion springs integrated directly at the joint. [10]
B. Electrical Architecture: To control a highly nonlinear, actuated mechanism, Optimus's electronic hardware system is designed with extreme sophistication using a distributed sensor network. At each finger joint, the system uses independent Hall effect sensors, combined with ring magnets placed directly around the joint's axis of rotation. This sensor structure allows the microcontroller to accurately read the actual angular position of each finger (proximal and distal), completely independent of the slack or tension of the pull cable. [10]

C. Control Architecture: Instead of using simple position control loops, Tesla Optimus's control algorithm applies an optimization framework to address the inherent instability of the adaptive finger mechanism. Based on the angle signal from the Hall sensor, the controller continuously calculates cable tension to achieve two optimization goals: (1) Minimizing post-contact work to minimize the displacement (slippage) of the object relative to the finger after grasping; and (2) Maximizing resistible external forces to maintain stable stability when the robot moves or collides with obstacles. [10]
Summary: The operating cycle of the Tesla Optimus hand is a perfect intersection of cybernetics and artificial intelligence. The process begins when the vision systemcombining a head-mounted camera and a close-up camera embedded in the palmcollects spatial and object imagery. This raw data stream is fed directly into an end-to-end neural network (running on an FSD chip), allowing the AI ??to automatically plan trajectories and output control signals without pre-programmed rules. High-performance motors located in the forearm respond, generating traction transmitted through a cable system (tendon-driven) to flex or extend the fingers with extreme flexibility (up to 22 DoF for Gen 3). As soon as the fingertips make contact with an object, tactile sensors immediately send data on pressure and contact location back to the brain. If the AI ??detects insufficient gripping force or a risk of the object slipping, it will continuously fine-tune the tension of each cable every few milliseconds to maintain an absolutely firm grip.
 1.3.3. Pneumatic & Soft Actuation Approach
General operating principle: Instead of using electric motors and rigid drive mechanisms, this method uses fluid pressure (usually compressed air) pumped into elastomeric chambers or artificial muscles (e.g., McKibben artificial muscles). [11] The pressure difference causes the material to expand asymmetrically, thereby creating the flexing movement of the finger. The clamping force generated is proportional to the compressed air pressure pumped into the effective surface of the chamber. Recently, researchers have also combined positive-negative pneumatic pressure to simulate both flexor and extensor muscles, increasing initial stiffness and shortening response time. [5]
Typical products analyzed:
Shadow Pneumatic Hand: Uses a McKibben pneumatic muscle network placed in the forearm to pull artificial tendons, almost perfectly mimicking the human musculoskeletal system. [11]
      
Figure 1.14: Shadow Pneumatic Hand
Soft-Rigid Hybrid Hand: Research by Zhang et al. (2023) combines a rigid internal skeleton and an external pneumatically actuated elastic shell (bellows), incorporating a strain-limiting nylon fabric layer to allow fingers to achieve large flexion angles (up to 259 degrees) and gripping forces up to 7.5N. [5]
GRACE Hand: A monolithic 3D-printed pneumatic hand using flexible artificial muscles that automatically adapt to the shape of objects. [4]

Figure 1.15: GRACE Hand
Evaluation and Design Consequences:
Superiority: Provides the highest level of intrinsic safety during physical interaction with humans due to the flexibility of the material. [4] Achieves an exceptionally high power-to-weight ratio in the finger portion. [12] Absolute shape adaptability. [4]
Core Limitations: The critical weakness preventing their application in commercially mobile humanoid robots is the bulkiness of the power source (including air compressors, reservoirs, solenoid valve networks, and pressure regulators). [12]Furthermore, due to non-linear dynamics, hysteresis of compressible fluids, and material deformation, applying precise closed-loop control algorithms (such as position/velocity PID control) is extremely difficult compared to electric motors.
 1.3.4. Direct-driven Approach
Operating principle: Completely eliminates intermediate transmission links (cables, bracing, belts). The electric motor (along with a micro-gearbox) is placed coaxially directly at the rotating joints of the fingers. Each degree of freedom is controlled independently by a separate motor (fully actuated). [6] [4]
Direct-drive hands eliminate intermediate transmission by connecting actuators directly to joints. This simplifies mechanical structure while still allowing for high actuatable DoFs, similar to tendon-driven designs.
Comparison and Evaluation:
Advantages: The mechanical structure is maximally simplified while maintaining a high number of degrees of freedom [2]. Thanks to the absence of intermediate drives, the control accuracy, sensitivity, and responsiveness of the system are very high.

Figure 1.16: a) Allergo Hand; b) KITECH-Hand
Disadvantages: The mass, rotational inertia, and size of the fingers are significantly increased. This bulkiness hinders agility in delicate manipulations, and this is why most direct-actuated hands are limited to a 4-finger configuration instead of 5 fingers.
While direct drive offers high control precision and respon-siveness, it introduces potential drawbacks such as increased mass, rotational inertia, and finger bulkiness, which may hinder agility in fine manipulation tasks. These limitations partly explain why most direct-drive hands adopt a four-finger configuration. [2]
 1.3.5. Hybrid-transmission Approach
Principles and Characteristics: Recognizing that no single method can solve the entire trade-off problem between power, flexibility, and size, many modern studies are turning to hybrid transmission design. This method combines the advantages of cables, tie rods, gears, or strategically distributed motors in one go.
 
Figure 1.17: a) Hu et al.s hand; b) LEAP Hand; c) The PUT- Hand
For example, the DLR/HIT Hand II and NAIST Hand use modular fingers with a combination of motors, belts, gears, tendon or linkage systems. The MCR-Hand series, utilize a linkage-tendon mixed transmission system to achieve compactness and high functionality. Adab Mora Hand , LEAP Hand V2 (DLA Hand) and Hu et al.s hand (see Fig. 3(e)) also integrate multiple transmission elements within fingers to enhance overall performance and adaptability.
Other hybrid designs explicitly differentiate mechanisms across fingers to match specific functional needs. The PUT-Hand (see Fig., for instance, combines a direct-drive thumb, linkage-driven index/middle fingers, and tendon-driven ring/little fingers. Similarly, the MPL v2.0 Hand, Tact Hand, and Six-DoF Open Source Hand adopt direct or geared actuation for the thumb while using tendons, linkages or timing belts for other fingers. Hands developed by Owen et al., Ryu et al., and Ke et al. follow a similar approach, implementing thumb-specific hybrid strategies to enhance opposability and dexterity.
Comparison and Evaluation:
Advantages: Offers the most perfect balance currently available: maintaining a slim profile (by pushing the large motor further away), self-adapting capability (thanks to the cable), and possessing very strong clamping force and flexible directional adjustment. [1]
Disadvantages: The complexity of the mechanical system and control algorithms is extremely high. For this system to operate accurately, it is necessary to develop "nonlinear Coulomb friction compensation" algorithms using pseudo-inverse matrices to eliminate force loss along the cable transmission path. [1]
 1.3.6. Topology Optimization / Computational Design Approach
Operating principle: Topology Optimization (TO) represents a shift from traditional mechanical design to computational design. Instead of engineers manually sketching the shapes of links and joints, this method uses mathematical algorithms combined with Finite Element Analysis (FEA) to automatically allocate and remove materials within a given design domain. [13]
The optimization process works by dividing the space into volumetric elements (voxels). The algorithm continuously iterates on the stiffness and deformation of each element under the influence of an input force, thereby deciding which elements to retain (material density approaching 1) and which to discard (density approaching 0) [13]. The end result is a structure with a shape that mimics biological evolution (bionic shape), optimizing the output motion (e.g., maximizing finger flexion) while maintaining material volume constraints. This method is particularly suitable for designing Compliant Mechanisms  mechanisms that transmit motion through the elastic deformation of the material itself instead of using rigid rotating joints.

Figure 1.18: Topology Optimization Design, b) Topology Optimization Working Principle
Typical examples:
3D Topology Optimized Compliant Finger: Sun and Lueth's (2021) study used the TO 3D algorithm in MATLAB to synthesize a monolithic robotic finger. Starting from a simple rectangular block, the algorithm "carved" and created a complex skeletal structure. When a force was applied at the base, this structure automatically bent to form a flexing trajectory similar to a human finger. The finger was then fabricated monolithically using Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) 3D printing technology with polyamide powder. [13]
Multimaterial Soft Fingers: Several other studies have used TO in combination with multimaterial 3D printing to design soft fingers actuated by pneumatics or cables. The algorithm will calculate the optimal distribution between areas of hard material (acting as bones) and soft material (acting as joints) to create adaptive grip patterns. [13]
Comparison and Evaluation:
Advantages:
Zero-assembly and high reliability: Because the structure is a seamless monolithic structure, it completely eliminates problems of joint friction, mechanical play (backlash), or cable breakage. Stress is evenly distributed along the length of the finger, minimizing mechanical fatigue.
Maximum weight optimization: Material is only placed where it is actually subjected to force, resulting in an excellent stiffness-to-weight ratio, extreme lightness, and high bio-aesthetic properties. [13]
Limitations:
Computational and theoretical complexity: Conventional rigid-link-based kinematics cannot be used to analyze or control this type of hand. A powerful computer system is required to run repeated FEA simulations hundreds of times in 3D space. [13]
Completely dependent on manufacturing technology: The shapes created by Topology Optimization often have extremely complex organic structures that cannot be machined using traditional methods such as CNC turning or milling. It absolutely requires advanced industrial 3D printing technologies (such as SLS or multi-material 3D printing) to realize them.
1.4. Thesis Objectives & Scope
 1.4.1. Generalization of the Problem from a Review of the Research Situation
From the analyses in the previous sections, it can be seen that current robotic arm platforms are facing a strict trade-off: fully-actuated systems offer high dexterity but are bulky and expensive [4]; while linkage-based systems are highly durable but lack flexibility and the ability to closely grip objects with complex shapes. [8] On the other hand, the cable-driven solution combined with underactuated mechanisms has proven to be the most optimal biomimetic approach, allowing large actuators to be moved away from the palm (towards the forearm) to reduce inertia and increase sensor integration space [1]. From this synthesis, the core problem is how to optimize the design of a low-conductivity cable drive system to ensure both compactness and the necessary flexibility for everyday handling and grasping.
 1.4.2. Research Objectives
The overall objective of this thesis is to research, design, and fabricate a biomimetic robotic hand system using a tendon-driven mechanism with a 10-degree-of-freedom (10-DOF) kinematic configuration.
The system is designed with an underactuated form to optimize the trade-off between dexterity and mechanical compactness. The hand aims for adaptive grasping based on object shape, utilizing 3D printing technology to minimize costs, and serving as a foundation for research into electromechanical prostheses or service robots. [1]

 2.1. Mechanical Architecture Proposal
 2.1.1. Proposal and Principle Explanation of the Primary Actuation Mechanism
To strictly adhere to the mass constraint of ? 500g and the static payload requirement of 1kg, this report proposes an Underactuated Tendon-driven Mechanism.
To determine the appropriate number of degrees of freedom (DoF), this thesis proposes three hypothetical configurations:
Configuration A (Biologically simulated full-force drive - 20 DoF): Each finger joint is controlled by an independent motor. Evaluation: Exceeds weight (< 500g) and installation space limits; low manufacturing feasibility.
Configuration B (Lack of full-force drive - 1 DoF): The entire hand uses a single motor to pull a common cable system (like the Pisa/IIT SoftHand platform). Evaluation: Has good object gripping capabilities but completely loses the ability for fine in-hand manipulation of each finger.
Configuration C (Independent drive combined with joint kinematic coupling - 10 DoF): Utilizing the biological kinematic coupling between the proximal interphalangeal joint (PIP) and the distal interphalangeal joint (DIP) in humans, these two joints are mechanically linked to move simultaneously. The four long fingers (Index, Middle, Ring, Little Finger) each possess 2 DoF (1 DoF for flexion/extension of the MCP joint, 1 DoF for flexion/extension of the PIP-DIP cluster). The thumb, however, possesses 2 independent DoFs (flexion/extension and abduction/adduction) to ensure adequate finger-to-finger space.
Conclusion of Selection: Through elimination testing, Configuration C (10-DoF Independent Actuation) is selected. This is a perfect trade-off: it eliminates the bulkiness of the 20-DoF configuration, while still allowing the controller (Cascaded PID) to independently intervene in each finger to perform precise grasping and pinching operations that the 1-DoF configuration cannot.

Operating Principle Explanation:
* Grasping Phase (Flexion): The motor actively spools the tendon. The resulting tension force (T) acts directly upon the offset point of the subsequent phalanx, generating a rotational moment around the static shaft. This force overcomes both the potential energy of the torsion springs and external object resistance, driving the finger into flexion. The tendon slides smoothly within the PTFE tubes, preventing abrasive wear on the primary load-bearing chassis.
* Passive Extension Phase: The motor initiates Active Unspooling to release the tendon. The pre-loaded torsion springs passively deploy, pushing the offset point to rotate in reverse around the static shaft. This retracts the finger to a fully extended state while maintaining continuous, dynamic tension on the tendon to prevent cable derailment at the spool.

 2.2.3. Critical Components & Solutions
To ensure stable operation of the electrical system within the extremely confined space of the palm of the hand, two key elements are proposed:
Electromagnetic Interference Shielding Layer (EMI Shielding Layer): To prevent magnetic fields radiated from the DC motor windings from distorting the microcontroller signal, the proposed system uses a physical shield. This layer acts as a Faraday cage, absorbing electromagnetic interference and discharging it to ground, creating an absolute protective boundary for the MCU and power ICs on the upper layer.
High-Performance Central MCU: Since a distributed multi-MCU architecture is no longer used, the palm-sized board will utilize a single 32-bit microcontroller (e.g., STM32G4 or ARM Cortex M4F, similar to the OPENGRASP-LITE platform [7]). This chip provides a sufficient number of hardware timers to handle interrupts from 10 square-phase encoder channels and output 10 PWM channels simultaneously, ensuring a PID (Cascaded Control) loop rate of 1 kHz without computational bottlenecks.
Actuator Unit Evaluation and Selection The selection of the actuation unit directly dictates the dynamic performance, grip strength, and overall mass of the palm module. Three micro-actuator paradigms are evaluated:
* Alternative 1: Micro RC Servo Motors (e.g., TowerPro MG90S).
o Pros: Integrates a DC motor, reduction gearbox, and position controller into a single package.
o Cons: Operates strictly in an open-loop paradigm (providing zero real-time position or torque telemetry to the main MCU). Furthermore, standard servos utilize non-backdrivable gear trains, making them highly prone to snapping tendons during unexpected rigid collisions.

* Alternative 2: Micro Stepper Motors.
o Pros: Delivers precise angular positioning and high holding torque without external sensors.
o Cons: Unfavorable power-to-weight ratio (mass penalty). Highly susceptible to "skipping steps" when subjected to sudden external loads during grasping, leading to cumulative, unrecoverable positional errors.

* Alternative 3: Micro DC Gearmotors with Quadrature Encoders.

o Pros: Exhibits exceptional power density and a compact form factor. The integrated magnetic encoder provides high-resolution, real-time feedbackan absolute prerequisite for implementing advanced closed-loop dynamic control (e.g., Cascaded PID).
o Cons: Demands external H-Bridge drivers and imposes a heavy computational burden on the MCU.
Selection Justification: Based on the evaluation matrix, Alternative 3 is unequivocally selected. Specifically, the design mandates the GA12-N20 DC Micro Gearmotor paired with a 1:150 Spur Gearbox. The decisive factor is the strict requirement for Mechanical Back-drivability. Unlike worm gearboxes (which are inherently self-locking) [7], the spur gear configuration permits the parallel torsion springs located at the distal phalangeal joints to actively pull the tendons backward during motor unspooling, preventing cable derailment inside the internal routing conduits.
 2.3. Control & Programming Proposal and Selection
By completely eliminating tactile sensors at the fingertips (to reduce weight) and utilizing a cable-driven mechanism from the palm, the ultimate goal of the control strategy is to indirectly control the cable tension to prevent structural fracture upon impact.
 2.3.1. Control Algorithm Alternatives
To solve the dynamics problem above, this thesis establishes and evaluates three hypotheses for the control algorithm:
Option 1: Classical Position PID control. This algorithm only tracks the angular trajectory from the encoder.
      
Critical limitation: When the finger collides with a hard object, the finger is stopped, but the position error continues to accumulate. This triggers "Integral Windup," pushing the duty cycle of the PWM pulse to a maximum of 100%. The inevitable consequence is that the motor will break the cable or destroy the 3D printed structure of the finger joints.
Option 2: Impedance Control. This algorithm calculates the inverse Jacobian matrix to deduce the necessary torque at the joint, simulating the natural stiffness and elasticity of muscles.

Critical Limitation: Although theoretically superior, its complex matrix operations create an excessive computational burden, making it impossible to implement at high frequencies (1 kHz) on low-end microcontrollers (STM32F1) located in the Slave modules.
Option 3: Cascade PID Control with Clamping Anti-Windup. The algorithm uses a nested loop architecture. The outer loop controls Position/Velocity, thereby outputting a reference value to the inner current loop. When the INA181 current sensor detects a stall current reaching a warning threshold due to object collision, the algorithm immediately "clamps" the integration stage to safely dampen the output PWM signal.

 2.3.2 Software Platform & IDE Selection
To synchronize and execute the Cascade PID algorithm at high frequencies (1 kHz) within the resource limitations of low-end microcontrollers (such as the STM32F1 in the Slave modules), the choice of software platform is crucial. This thesis evaluates two software architecture options as follows:

 3.3. Design, Evaluation, and Selection of Passive Extension Mechanism
The robotic arm system uses a unidirectional cable-driven (Tendon-driven) mechanism. Therefore, the finger extension phase depends entirely on the elastic potential energy of the return mechanism system. The goal is to design a system that provides a maximum spring_maximum    =60.0 N.mm at the joint (equivalent to generating a maximum counter-cable tension F_spring?10.0 N when the finger is flexed to a maximum of 90°).
To solve this problem, the thesis proposes and conducts a comparative analysis of three elastic mechanism options: Torsion Spring, Sheathed Spring, and Rubber/TPU Elastomeric Ligament.

Option 1: Torsion Spring
Configuration: Uses a stainless steel spring placed directly at the pivot point of the joint. Wire diameter d=0.5 mm, inner diameter D_in ?=4.0 mm, 3 coils. [15]
Mechanical characteristics: The coiled spring has linear force-displacement characteristics. Advantages include fast response, high fatigue strength, and stable operation.However, the core disadvantage is that the initial pretension force when the finger is in the extended state is very small, easily leading to slackening. [15]
Option 2: Axial compression/tension spring (Sheathed Spring)
Configuration: Uses a cylindrical spring (wire diameter 0.4 mm, outer diameter 2.8 mm, length 17 mm) embedded in a hollow sheath designed inside the 3D-printed finger.
Mechanical characteristics: Similar to a coil spring, the cylindrical spring provides linear force. The embedded design allows for a compact structure and protects the spring from environmental influences. However, sliding friction between the spring wall and the 3D-printed sheath reduces the force transmission efficiency, and assembly is extremely complex.
Option 3: Artificial elastic ligament (TPU 95A / Rubber Ligament)
Configuration: Inspired by the structure of the human extensor tendon (Extensor Tendon), this design uses strips of super-elastic material (TPU 95A or rubber) attached to the dorsal side of the fingers.The TPU strips, with a thickness of 0.8 mm to 1.5 mm, are designed with two ends that expand and a middle section that narrows to optimize stress distribution.
Mechanical properties: TPU 95A material can withstand tensile stress up to 29.0 MPa and elongation at break of 330%.Unlike metal springs, TPU ligaments possess a nonlinear stress-strain curve.It provides a very large initial pulling force, helping to maintain excellent pretension even when the finger is fully extended.
Proposed Selection: Hybrid Extension Mechanism
To eliminate the disadvantages and maximize the advantages of each material, this thesis decides to choose a hybrid solution: Simultaneously using stainless steel coil springs (0.5mm wire diameter) at the joint axis combined with TPU 95A ligaments (0.8mm - 1.5mm thick) bonded to the finger surface.
This combination offers three groundbreaking technical advantages:
Mechanical Complementarity: The total elastic resistance force generated is a combination of linear and nonlinear equations:
F_spring=F_torsion+F_rubber
In the initial phase of the movement (when the finger is first bent), the rubber ligament provides instantaneous tension (initial pretension) to counteract slack in the traction cable. In the deep flexion phase (large angle), the torsion spring bears most of the resistance torque, preventing the rubber strip from being overstretched and causing material slack.
Establishing Natural Sequential Flexion: By varying the pre-stretch length of the rubber ligaments at different joints, we can establish higher assumed stiffness for the DIP joint and lower stiffness for the MCP joint. This hybrid structure forces the tendon-driven system to flex in the correct anatomical sequence: flexing the MCP root first, then gradually curling the PIP and DIP joints, helping to perfectly envelop the object's shape.
Palmar cartilage simulation - Anti-reverse flexion (Volar Plate Biomimicry): The TPU strip is designed to mimic the palmar cartilage disc and extensor tendons in humans.When the robotic hand accidentally impacts a table surface, the metal spring may detach, but the 95A TPU ligament will completely absorb the impact, limiting the reverse flexion angle (hyperextension) to a safe level (approximately 20°), protecting the 3D-printed plastic hinge pins from cracking.
Conclusion: This hybrid structure ensures that the total reaction torque always approaches the design level of 60 N.mm (F_spring=10 N), providing absolute smoothness for the Vectran/Dyneema cable system while maintaining the industrial durability of the mechanical structure. This is a solid input configuration for solving the Euler-Eytelwein friction loss problem in the next dynamics step.

 3.9.3. Bearing Tolerance & Fits:
For the screw assembly to operate stably, without optical misalignment and without ball jamming, the positioning tolerances between the inner bearing ring and the shaft, and the outer bearing ring and the housing (3D-printed plastic) must strictly comply with TCVN 1482-85 standard.
The thesis conducts a load characteristic analysis to select the mounting type:
Assemble the inner ring with the screw shaft (?3 mm):
Load characteristics: The inner ring rotates with the screw shaft, and radial forces act alternately across the entire raceway. This is a cyclic load. [17]
Select the mounting type: To prevent the rolling ring from shifting and wearing out, the inner ring must be fitted tightly with interference. Refer to Table 4.1 (TCVN 2245-99), for a shaft diameter d<100 mm subjected to cyclic loading, the tolerance range of the shaft is selected as k6. [17]
Assemble the outer ring with the gearbox housing (?7 mm):
Load characteristics: The outer ring remains stationary within the housing's support, with the force concentrated only on a portion of the rolling path. This is a local load. [17]
Select the mounting type: To allow the outer ring to move along the micro-axis due to thermal expansion and to facilitate easy disassembly and maintenance, a loose fit with clearance is required. Referring to Table 4.1, for a monolithic housing, the tolerance range for the housing bore is selected as H7. [17]
Conclusion: On the manufacturing drawings and 3D printed models, the screw bearing system will be set up with the following tolerance series: The machined screw shaft meets the standard ?3 k6 (tolerance from +0.002 to +0.008 mm) and the bearing hole on the 3D printed housing meets the standard ?7 H7 (tolerance from 0 to+0.015 mm).  3.10. Design Conclusion
The dynamic analysis and mechanical structure synthesis process in Chapter 3.1 successfully transformed the general functional requirements into a set of quantitative system specifications. The Hybrid Actuation Architecture, incorporating a Bowden tube (Teflon tube) mechanism, was chosen as the optimal solution to thoroughly address the conflicting issues of space, load, and dynamic disturbances.The system was finalized with the following core technical specifications:
Design Validation: 
Load-bearing capacity: Grasp line provides a pulling force of 44.8 N, creating a gripping force of 2.0 N/finger (responsive to a static load of 1 kg with the entire hand)
Response speed: The Teflon tube isolates the cable trajectory from the MCP root joint, shortening the time to full finger closure to 1.82 s. The MCP joint completes positional setup in 0.91 s.
Engineering Trade-offs:
Space vs. Speed: To make the module fit comfortably in the palm of the hand, the design uses a 1:1000 gearbox. Sacrificing finger-squeezing speed (1.82 s) for high clamping force on an extremely low motor power (?0.15 W) is a necessary physical trade-off.
Performance vs. Self-locking: The Pose Gland using a double-threaded screw boosts performance by approximately 50% and increases speed, but at the cost of losing the absolute self-locking feature.
Control Handoff Constraint:
To compensate for mechanical limitations, the Embedded System (Control Chapter) MUST develop two closed-loop algorithms:
Current Clamping: Actively limits current, immediately interrupting PWM when the Grasp line load approaches a dangerous threshold to protect the gearbox 1:1000 against shock loads.
Position Holding PID: Runs a PID loop providing continuous holding current to the Pose motor to hold the shaft, counteracting backdriven worm gears.

 4.10 Chapter Summary
This chapter presented the complete electrical design of the bionic Hand system, organized as a traceable derivation chain from mechanical requirements to component selection decisions. The key conclusions are summarized as follows. 
Motor Selection: Two GA12-N20 DC gearmotors are selected per finger  a 1:150 ratio Grasp motor (K1) providing 480 RPM output and sufficient tendon pull force for grasping, and a 1:1000 ratio Pose motor (K2) providing 15 RPM with 10.0 kg·cm stall torque for precise joint positioning. Both variants integrate a 7 CPR magnetic encoder, yielding effective outputshaft resolutions of 4,200 counts/rev (K1) and 28,000 counts/rev (K2) under Ũ4 quadrature counting.
Motor Driver: The TB6612FNG dual H-bridge (Toshiba, SSOP-24) is selected as the motor driver for each of the 5 Slave boards. It satisfies all 12 selection criteria  notably the 3.2 A peak current rating (3.2Ũ safety margin over stall current), direct 3.3 V logic compatibility, integrated TSD and STBY functions, and a compact footprint enabling PCB mounting inside the finger housing. PWM frequency is set to 20 kHz with 11.8-bit resolution for silent, smooth motor control. 
Communication Protocol: RS485 with Modbus RTU at 500 kbps is selected for MasterSlave inter-node communication. This baud rate is derived analytically from the 1 kHz PID sampling requirement and provides a safety factor of 1.87Ũ over the minimum required rate. Differential RS485 signaling provides ą7 V common-mode rejection against motor PWM noise. The MAX485ESA+ transceiver is selected for its 3.3 V compatibility, 2.5 Mbps maximum rate, and ą15 kV HBM ESD protection. 
Microcontroller Selection: A two-tier Master-Slave MCU architecture is adopted. The STM32F407VGT6 (168 MHz, Cortex-M4F with FPU, 6 UARTs, USB OTG) serves as the Master, executing kinematic decoupling and outer-loop PID at 100 Hz. Five STM32F103C8T6 units (72 MHz, Cortex-M3, hardware QEI timers) serve as Slave controllers, each executing 2 inner-loop PIDs at 1 kHz and managing local encoder counting and PWM generation. 
Power Supply: A 3-Cell Lithium Polymer (LiPo 3S) battery pack  11.1 V nominal, 2,200 mAh, ~200 Wh/kg energy density  is selected as the primary energy source for the 12 V motor domain. The LiPo chemistry offers the best weight-to-energy trade-off among all evaluated battery technologies and supports over 1.3 hours of continuous operation at nominal load. A dual-domain power architecture separates the motor supply (12 V, via LTC3780 BuckBoost module) from the logic supply (3.3 V, via AMS1117-3.3V LDO from USB 5 V). This isolation prevents PWM switching transients from corrupting the MCU and encoder logic rails, avoids excessive LDO thermal dissipation, and enables independent signal-domain powering during firmware development. 
The consolidated system power budget confirms a nominal consumption of ? 19.9 W and peak of ? 37.9 W (3-motor stall scenario), both within the ratings of the selected LTC3780 and LiPo battery. All 15 BOM line items are commercially available and within the student capstone budget. The electrical design is fully traceable to the mechanical requirements established in Chapter 3, providing a solid foundation for the control and firmware design presented in Chapter 5.


CHAPTER 5: Kinematic and Control Design
 5.1  Chapter Overview
The Hand robotic system presents a set of control challenges that cannot be addressed by conventional single-loop position controllers. The mechanical architecture an underactuated, tendon-driven finger with two motors commanding three coupled joints  introduces three interrelated problems that the control layer must simultaneously resolve:
 Kinematic Cross-Coupling: The grasp cable (Cable 2) passes through the MCP pulley before reaching the PIP and DIP joints. Any rotation of the MCP joint (controlled by Motor 1) changes the effective free length of Cable 2, disturbing the PIP/DIP position commanded by Motor 2. Without compensation, the two motors interfere with each other.
 Underactuated Constraint: The system has three joints (MCP, PIP, DIP) but only two independent actuators. The DIP joint angle is passively constrained to follow the PIP joint through a fixed mechanical coupling ratio determined by the spring stiffness and pulley geometry. A controller that ignores this constraint will command physically unrealizable trajectories.
 Cable Integrity Protection: Unlike rigid linkages, tendons can only pull, not push. An integral windup event  where the position error accumulates against a stalled motor  drives the PWM output to 100% duty cycle, generating peak current sufficient to snap a Vectran cable or strip the 3D-printed spool. The controller must include an active protection mechanism.
To address all three problems within the computational budget of the STM32F103 slave microcontroller (72 MHz, 20 KB SRAM), this chapter presents a Model-Based, Cascade Position-Velocity PID control architecture organized around four design layers:
(1) Kinematic & Static Model  Derives the Tendon Routing Matrix and establishes the underactuated coupling ratio from first principles (Section 5.3).
(2) Kinematic Decoupler  A feedforward compensation block that isolates the motor commands, eliminating cross-coupling between Motor 1 and Motor 2 (Section 5.4).
(3) Cascade PID Controller  A two-loop structure (outer position loop, inner velocity loop) with Anti-Windup clamping for cable protection (Section 5.5).
(4) System State Machine & Pseudo-code  A Finite State Machine (FSM) governing the hand-level operation cycle, with pseudo-code for both Master and Slave MCU firmware (Sections 5.65.7).
The control design philosophy adopted in this thesis prioritizes structural robustness over theoretical optimality: every algorithm was selected to guarantee safe operation under worst-case mechanical disturbances (cable slack, torsion spring nonlinearity, Coulomb friction in the PTFE conduit) while remaining implementable within the hard real-time deadline of T_PID  = 1 ms.

 Coordinate Frame Selection Rationale
The choice of coordinate frame origin is deliberately adapted to the purpose of each chapter. In Chapter 3, the kinematic model is anchored at the MCP joint of each finger. This local-frame convention is the natural choice for mechanical design: all dimensional parameters  proximal phalanx length l?, middle phalanx l?, distal phalanx l?  are measured directly from the MCP pivot, which is the physical reference point on the fabricated part. Workspace boundaries, joint angle limits, and FEA load cases are all defined relative to this local origin, allowing each finger to be designed, simulated, and validated in isolation without reference to where it will be mounted on the palm. The resulting 3-DOF planar model (? = 0 at every frame) is algebraically compact and computationally lightweight, making it suitable for manual calculation and CAD verification.
In Chapter 5, however, the controller must coordinate all five fingers simultaneously within a single shared task space. A local MCP frame that differs for each finger would require the Master MCU to maintain five separate, inconsistent coordinate systems  introducing unnecessary transformation overhead at every 100 Hz planning cycle. By re-establishing the kinematic chain from the global palm frame {O} using the full 5-frame DH convention (Frames 12 fixed, Frames 35 joints), all fingertips are expressed in one unified coordinate system. This enables the trajectory planner to reason about inter-finger relationships directly  for example, computing the thumb-to-index overlap needed for a precision pinch, or verifying that two fingers approach the same object surface from geometrically consistent directions  without any runtime coordinate conversion.  5.5.  Cascade Position-Velocity PID Controller Design
 5.5.1  Controller Architecture Overview

Figure 5.3: Motor control block diagram: cascaded position, velocity controller
The control architecture implements a two-level cascade loop running entirely on the Slave MCU at 1 kHz. This separation of control layers is motivated by three engineering principles:
* Bandwidth Separation: The inner velocity loop (bandwidth ? 50 Hz) responds 50Ũ faster than the outer position loop (bandwidth ? 1 Hz). The inner loop rejects motor friction and inertia disturbances before they propagate to the position error, dramatically reducing the position controller's tuning difficulty.
* Physical Unit Transparency: The signal flow maintains consistent physical units at each node  joint space [rad] ? decoupler ? motor space [rad] ? position error [rad] ?  5.5.4  Anti-Windup Clamping  Cable Protection Mechanism
When a finger closes around a rigid object, the motor stalls (?_act  ? 0 while ?_ref  > 0). In a standard PI controller, the integral term I_vel (k) continues to accumulate, driving PWM to 100% and sustained stall current to I_stall  = 1.0 A. At this current level, the Vectran cable (breaking load > 300 N) is safe, but the 3D-printed PETG spool and the N20 motor winding overheat within ? 30 seconds. This is unacceptable for sustained grasping tasks.
The Anti-Windup Clamping algorithm prevents integral accumulation by freezing the integrator whenever the PWM output reaches a saturation threshold:
If |PWM(k)|>= PWM_ma   THEN  I_vel (k) = I_vel(k-1)     (no update)
ELSE  I_vel(k) = I_vel(k-1) + Ki_vel· e_vel(k)   · Ts
The saturation threshold is set to PWM_max  = 90% (not 100%), providing a 10% headroom that corresponds to a maximum sustained current of approximately 0.9 A  well below the motor's thermal limit. When the PWM is clamped, the controller naturally transitions from position tracking mode to constant-force holding mode, maintaining a firm grip on the object without further stressing the mechanical structure.
Additionally, a current monitoring check via software-estimated duty-cycle is implemented: if PWM remains saturated for longer than T_(hold_max )  = 5 seconds (configurable via Modbus register), the Slave firmware automatically reduces PWM to a holding PWM level of 30%, sufficient to maintain grip torque against the torsion springs without thermal risk.

 5.7.  Algorithm Flowchart and Pseudo-code
This section presents the flowcharts describing the firmware structure implemented on the microcontroller. The overall control system is organized into two parallel execution layers: a background main loop that handles communication and state transitions, and a timer-triggered Interrupt Service Routine (ISR) that executes the dual-loop PID algorithm at a fixed frequency of 1 kHz. The five flowcharts below describe, respectively: the main control loop, the homing routine, the outer position controller, the inner velocity PI controller, and the S-curve trajectory generator.
 5.7.1. Main control loop
Figure 5.4 illustrates the top-level firmware structure. Upon startup, the system performs two sequential initialization steps: (1) hardware configuration and loading of control parameters into memory, and (2) execution of the homing routine to establish the zero reference position for all joints (detailed in Section 5.7.2).
After homing completes, the program enters the main loop, marked by connector node A. This loop operates on a timer-polling mechanism: at each iteration, the controller checks the timer interrupt flag; if the 1 ms sampling period has not elapsed, the loop continues to wait. Once the timer fires, the following tasks are executed in sequence:
1. Read encoder feedback  acquire the actual joint angle of each motor via the onboard encoder. 
2. Get reference trajectory  retrieve the reference setpoint qrefq_{\mathrm{ref}} qref? generated by the S-curve trajectory block (Section 5.7.5). 
3. Static Decoupler  transform joint-space coordinates into tendon-space, compensating for the kinematic coupling inherent in the 2-tendon 3-joint mechanism. 
4. Outer Position Loop  execute the P controller to compute the velocity setpoint for the inner loop (Section 5.7.3). 
5. Inner Velocity Loop  execute the PI controller to compute the raw PWM command (Section 5.7.4).
Once the PWM value is computed, the system evaluates the actuator saturation condition: if PWM>95%, the Anti-windup clamp branch is activated to prevent integrator wind-up; otherwise, the integrator is updated normally (Update Integrator). The resulting PWM value is then written to the H-bridge driver (TB6612NG) to drive the DC motors.

 5.7.2. Homing Routine
Figure 5.5 describes the homing procedure, which is called exactly once immediately after hardware initialization. The objective of this routine is to drive all motors to their respective mechanical hard stops  corresponding to the fully extended finger posture  thereby establishing a zero encoder reference for the entire system.
The routine begins by initializing homing parameters, then commands each motor to rotate in the RETRACT direction (tendon retraction, corresponding to finger extension). At every sampling cycle, the motor velocity is read and checked against the condition v_motor<0: if the motor is still moving, the loop repeats. When a motor reaches its mechanical hard stop, its velocity drops to zero and the above condition is met.
To prevent false detection due to transient disturbances, a consecutive-count filter is applied: the motor must register zero velocity for at least 5 consecutive cycles (equivalent to 5 ms) before the mechanical stop is confirmed. Once all motors have successfully completed homing, the encoders are reset to zero and the system is ready to enter the main control loop.

 5.9  Chapter Summary
This chapter has presented the complete control and programming design for the Hand robotic system. The design process followed a model-first methodology: mathematical models established in Chapters 3 and 4 were translated into quantitative control design parameters, ensuring every gain, threshold, and timing constant is traceable to a physical requirement.
The key contributions of this chapter are:
* Design Traceability (Section 5.2): A complete parameter traceability table links mechanical, electrical, and kinematic results from Chapters 14 to their corresponding control design implications, demonstrating the integrated multi-domain design methodology required by modern mechatronic systems.
* Model-Based Foundation (Section 5.3): The Tendon Routing Matrix R ? ?^(2Ũ3) was derived from first principles, establishing the kinematic coupling ratio c = 0.83 from the spring-spring balance equation  a physically meaningful constraint that eliminates one degree of freedom from the control problem.
* Kinematic Decoupler (Section 5.4): The Static Decoupler, implemented as two linear equations (Eq. 5.8a/5.8b), eliminates the cross-coupling between MCP and PIP/DIP degrees of freedom. This transforms the multi-input, coupled problem into two independent SISO control loops, dramatically simplifying controller tuning.
* Cascade PID with Anti-Windup (Section 5.5): The two-loop cascade architecture (outer P position loop at 100 Hz, inner PI velocity loop at 1 kHz) achieves bandwidth separation of 20Ũ. The Anti-Windup clamping mechanism (PWM_max  = 90%) protects the tendon and motor from overload during grasping contact, transitioning smoothly from position tracking to force-holding mode.
* Finite State Machine (Section 5.6): A six-state FSM governs the complete hand operation cycle from initialization through grasping to emergency stop, providing deterministic, safe behavior under all operating conditions including communication faults.
* Flowchart and Timing Analysis (Sections 5.75.8): Complete pseudo-code for both Master (STM32F407) and Slave (STM32F103) firmware was presented, with a verified ISR execution budget of ? 5.3 ĩs  just 0.53% of the 1 ms control period  confirming the feasibility of the real-time architecture.

The control design satisfies all three challenge requirements identified in Section 5.1: cross-coupling is eliminated by the decoupler; the underactuated constraint is embedded as a fixed mechanical ratio in the firmware; and cable integrity is protected by the Anti-Windup clamping and hold-mode logic. The results provide the quantitative foundation for the simulation and experimental validation presented in Chapter 6.
